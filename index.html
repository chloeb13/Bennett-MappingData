Climate resilience funds could go toward an unlikely place: Abandoned mines

Visit the town of Ramapo, New York and you will find a blue lake surrounded by grass and mature trees. But the area hasn’t always been green.

For years the 38-acre land was used as a basalt mine. Then in 2006, the mine’s owner donated the land to Ramapo for recreational use.

The Mined Land Reclamation Law requires that expired mines apply for a reclamation permit, which ensures the land will be returned to a natural state after excavation. In New York, 3,198 mines have been reclaimed since 1974, and 34 are expired and eligible but have not begun the reclamation process. There is a total of 6,026 mines in the state.

A $1.2 trillion infrastructure bill that aims toward improving transportation, water systems and broadband was signed into law. Almost 5% of the bill is marked for climate resiliency projects, which includes mine reclamation.

Mine reclamation normally takes around two years, Todd Pignataro, spokesperson for the NY Department of Environmental Conservation said.

Environmental impact

Most mining sites in the state produce materials like salt and gravel. Reclamation of mining sites results in several ecologically positive impacts.

“It can improve wildlife habitat, promote plant diversity, and prevent adverse environmental impacts like soil erosion and sediment runoff into neighboring water bodies,” Catherine Dickert, director of mineral resources for the department said. “Final mine reclamation ensures the land is stabilized, restored, and returned to productive use in a way that’s protective of the environment.”

While it remains too early to know when and how the money will be distributed, the state could see a faster turnaround for reclamation projects in major mining sites.
